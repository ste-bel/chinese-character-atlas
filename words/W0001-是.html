W0001 — 是

1 🔊

Pronunciation

是 is pronounced shì — fourth tone, falling sharply from high to low.

The fourth tone is decisive. Say it the way you would answer "yes" emphatically in English. The tone mirrors the word's meaning: affirmation, certainty, identification.

Tone change: 不是 (bú shì — is not) — when 不 precedes a fourth-tone syllable, it shifts to second tone: bú instead of bù.

2 📖

Meaning

是 is the Chinese verb "to be" — but only in one specific sense: identity and classification.

It answers the question: What kind of thing is A?

A 是 B — A is B. A belongs to the category B. A equals B in definition.

A is the subject. B is a noun or noun phrase. 是 links them.

When NOT to use 是:

是 is not a universal "to be." Three different verbs handle what English calls "to be":

Function Chinese Example
Identity: A is B 是 (shì) 他是老师 — He is a teacher
Location: A is at B 在 (zài) 他在家 — He is at home
Existence/Possession: A has B 有 (yǒu) 他有时间 — He has time

This is one of the most important distinctions in Mandarin. English "to be" does all three. Chinese does not.

◆ Atlas Note

The word 是 originally meant correct, right, this one — a demonstrative pointing to something as the true or proper thing. Its use as a copula ("to be") developed naturally: to say something is correct, is the right thing, is to say it is what it is. Accuracy became existence became identity.

3 ✎

Examples

Beginner

我是学生。 Wǒ shì xuésheng. I am a student. Literal: I / am / student.

她是老师。 Tā shì lǎoshī. She is a teacher. Literal: She / is / teacher.

这是书。 Zhè shì shū. This is a book. Literal: This / is / book.

我是加拿大人。 Wǒ shì Jiānádà rén. I am Canadian. Literal: I / am / Canada / person.

Intermediate

这不是我的问题。 Zhè bú shì wǒ de wèntí. This is not my problem. Literal: This / is-not / my / problem.

他是我最好的朋友。 Tā shì wǒ zuì hǎo de péngyou. He is my best friend. Literal: He / is / my / most good / [de] / friend.

中文是一门很美的语言。 Zhōngwén shì yī mén hěn měi de yǔyán. Chinese is a very beautiful language. Literal: Chinese / is / one [measure] / very beautiful / [de] / language.

Advanced

这幅画是唐代的作品。 Zhè fú huà shì Táng dài de zuòpǐn. This painting is a work from the Tang dynasty. Literal: This [measure] painting / is / Tang dynasty [de] / work.

问题是他根本没来。 Wèntí shì tā gēnběn méi lái. The problem is that he simply didn't come. Literal: Problem / is / he / fundamentally / did-not / come. Grammar note: 是 can introduce a clause that defines the subject — "the problem is that…"

4 ？

Questions

是 appears naturally in yes/no questions using the particle 吗 (ma) at the end.

你是中国人吗？ Nǐ shì Zhōngguó rén ma? Are you Chinese?

✅ 是的，我是中国人。 Shì de, wǒ shì Zhōngguó rén. Yes, I am Chinese.

❌ 不是，我不是中国人。我是加拿大人。 Bú shì, wǒ bú shì Zhōngguó rén. Wǒ shì Jiānádà rén. No, I am not Chinese. I am Canadian.

Alternative: 是不是 (shì bú shì) — a more direct question form, expecting confirmation:

他是不是你的老师？ Tā shì bú shì nǐ de lǎoshī? Is he or isn't he your teacher? / He's your teacher, right?

5 ▦

Grammar

Pattern 1 — Basic identity

Subject + 是 + Noun

The most fundamental pattern. 是 links a subject to a noun that classifies or identifies it.

我是学生 → I am a student 北京是首都 → Beijing is the capital

Pattern 2 — Negation: 不是 (bú shì)

Subject + 不是 + Noun

Insert 不 before 是 to negate.

他不是老师 → He is not a teacher 这不是真的 → This is not true

Note: 不 (bù) changes to second tone (bú) before any fourth-tone syllable, including 是.

Pattern 3 — Emphasis: 就是 (jiù shì)

Subject + 就是 + Noun

就 adds emphasis: "precisely," "exactly," "none other than."

他就是那个人 → He is precisely that person. 这就是问题所在 → This is exactly where the problem lies.

Pattern 4 — Concessive: 是…但是 (shì… dànshì)

Statement + 是 + repetition + 但是 + contrast

A sophisticated pattern that acknowledges a fact before introducing a counterpoint. Common in spoken Mandarin.

他聪明是聪明，但是不努力。 He is indeed smart, but he doesn't work hard. Literal: He smart is smart, but not hardworking.

Common constructions with 是

Expression Pinyin Meaning
是的 shì de yes; that's right
不是 bú shì is not; no
就是 jiù shì exactly; precisely
可是 kě shì but; however
于是 yú shì therefore; as a result
还是 hái shì still; or (in questions)
总是 zǒng shì always
只是 zhǐ shì only; merely; just
要是 yào shì if; supposing

Adjectives do NOT follow 是

This is the single most common beginner mistake. In Chinese, adjectives act as their own predicates. They do not need 是.

❌ 我是高兴 (unnatural — do not say this) ✅ 我很高兴 — I am happy (very + adjective = neutral statement) ✅ 我高兴 — I am happy (adjective alone = emphasis or specific context)

6 ⬡

Character Analysis

是 is composed of two parts:

日 (rì) — Sun; Day (top component → CMP0001 日)

The sun appears at the top of 是. In oracle bone script, 日 was a circle with a dot at center — the sun with its radiant core. The presence of 日 in 是 connects the word to ideas of clarity, correctness, and the light of truth.

正 (zhèng) — Correct; Upright (lower component)

The lower portion of 是 evolved from 正, meaning upright, correct, right. The combination is meaningful: the sun (clarity, illumination) over correctness — what is lit clearly by the sun is what is true.

The character CH0001 是 — 是 — is examined in full detail on its character page.

7 ⌛

Historical Evolution

The story of 是 is a story about pointing and truth.

Oracle Bone Script (甲骨文) — ~1200 BCE

In the earliest inscriptions, 是 depicted the sun (日) above a foot or a step. The meaning was demonstrative: this one, this here, right here. The foot indicated pointing or stepping toward something — here is the thing itself.

Bronze Script (金文) — ~1000 BCE

The foot component gradually shifted toward the form that would become 正 (upright, correct). The word began acquiring its sense of rightness — not just "this thing" but "the correct thing."

Seal Script (篆书) — ~200 BCE

By the time of the Qin dynasty's standardization, 是 had settled into the sun-over-correct-foot form. The demonstrative meaning was fading; the identity meaning was sharpening.

Clerical Script (隶书) — Han dynasty

The brush strokes were flattened and systematized. The sun and the lower component became clearly recognizable as they appear today.

Regular Script (楷书) — Tang dynasty to present

The form stabilized into the 是 we write today. The meaning is now almost exclusively copulative: A is B. The ancient pointing sense survives only in classical texts and set expressions.

8 🔊

Pronunciation in Depth

Standard Mandarin: shì — fourth tone Taiwan (Guoyü): shì — identical to mainland standard Cantonese: 係 (hai6) — a different character and word serves the same function in Cantonese

Regional Mandarin variation: The retroflex quality of sh varies significantly by region. Speakers from the north (Beijing, Harbin) pronounce it with a strong retroflex curl. Speakers from the south (Guangdong, Fujian) may produce a softer sound.

Historical pronunciation: In Middle Chinese (中古汉语), the ancestor of Mandarin, 是 was pronounced approximately dʑiɪ, with a voiced initial. The falling tone reflects what was originally a "entering tone" syllable. Over centuries, the pronunciation shifted dramatically from its classical form.

9 📖

Modern Usage

Formal/Written: 是 appears constantly in formal writing — documents, news, literature. It is neutral in register.

Spoken/Informal: In casual speech, 是 sometimes shortens or blends. 是的 (shì de — yes) is extremely common. Younger speakers sometimes emphasize with 就是 (jiù shì) as an intensifier.

Internet/Digital: 是的 → 是哒 (shì dā) — a softer, cuter affirmation common in online text. 就是就是 (jiù shì jiù shì) — emphatic agreement, common in social media.

Classical Chinese: In wenyan (文言文), 是 was commonly used as a demonstrative pronoun (this, this one) rather than a copula. The sentence 是吾师也 means "this is my teacher" — 是 here is the demonstrative subject, not the verb.

10 ⚠

Common Mistakes

English interference

English speakers overuse 是 because they translate "to be" directly.

Wrong Right Why
❌ 他是在家 ✅ 他在家 Location uses 在, not 是
❌ 我是好 ✅ 我很好 Adjectives are predicates
❌ 那是很贵 ✅ 那很贵 That is expensive → adjective

French interference

French speakers make the same "to be" over-transfer (être is also used for adjectives).

❌ 这是美丽 → ✅ 这很美丽 (or: 这很美) C'est beau does not translate directly to 这是美. The adjective stands alone.

Spanish/Italian interference

Spanish es/está maps to 是 and 在 respectively — two distinct concepts that learners conflate.

Él es maestro → 他是老师 ✅ (identity → 是) Él está en casa → 他在家 ✅ (location → 在, not 是)

The distinction between ser and estar in Spanish is actually a useful analogy: 是 is more like ser (permanent identity), and 在 is more like estar (location, state).

11 ⛰

Cultural Notes

是 in Classical Philosophy

The Mohists (墨家, ~400 BCE) engaged in intense philosophical debate about 是 and 非 — right/correct and wrong/incorrect. The pair 是非 (shì fēi) became a fundamental philosophical opposition: what is, what is not; right and wrong; truth and falsehood. This pair appears constantly in classical Chinese thought.

是 in Modern Culture

是的 (shì de) is one of the most common polite affirmations in Mandarin conversation. The addition of 的 (de) softens the plain 是 and makes it feel considered rather than abrupt — more like "yes, that is so" than a clipped "yes."

是 in Idioms and Set Phrases

是可忍，孰不可忍？ Shì kě rěn, shú bù kě rěn? If this can be tolerated, what cannot be tolerated? — Confucius, Analects (论语), a famous expression of moral outrage.

The 是 here is demonstrative: this can be tolerated — can this be tolerated? The phrase became a classical expression of righteous indignation.

◆ Atlas Note

English uses one verb — "to be" — to handle identity, location, existence, state, and description. Chinese splits these functions across 是 (identity), 在 (location), and 有 (existence/possession). This is not a limitation of Chinese. It is a precision that English lacks.

When you say 我是加拿大人, you are making a statement of identity: I belong to the category of Canadians. When you say 我在加拿大, you are making a statement of location: I exist within Canadian space. These are genuinely different propositions. Chinese grammar forces you to be clear about which one you mean.

A learner who truly internalizes this distinction has begun to think in Chinese — not merely translating from English.
