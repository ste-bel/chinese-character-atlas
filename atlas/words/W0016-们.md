# W0016 — 们

## Pronunciation

**们**
**Pinyin:** men (neutral tone — unstressed)
**Meaning:** plural suffix for pronouns and people

们 is always unstressed. It is a grammatical suffix, not an independent word.

## Core Usage

们 attaches to personal pronouns and certain nouns referring to people to make them plural:

| Singular | Plural | Meaning |
|----------|--------|---------|
| 我 (wǒ) | 我们 (wǒmen) | we; us |
| 你 (nǐ) | 你们 (nǐmen) | you all |
| 他 (tā) | 他们 (tāmen) | they; them (male/mixed) |
| 她 (tā) | 她们 (tāmen) | they; them (female) |

们 can also attach to nouns referring to people in informal or affectionate speech:

孩子们 — the children
同学们 — classmates (plural address)
朋友们 — friends (plural address)

## Examples

{{audio:我们是朋友。}}
我们是朋友。
*Wǒmen shì péngyou.*
We are friends.
Literal: We / are / friend(s).

{{audio:你们好！}}
你们好！
*Nǐmen hǎo!*
Hello everyone! (to a group)
Literal: You all / good!

{{audio:他们在哪里？}}
他们在哪里？
*Tāmen zài nǎlǐ?*
Where are they?

## Important Limitation

们 can only be used with pronouns and nouns for people. It cannot pluralise things.

✅ 老师们 (teachers — people)
❌ 书们 (wrong — objects do not take 们)

Chinese nouns are already number-neutral. 书 means both "book" and "books."

## Common Mistake

English speakers sometimes avoid 们 because Chinese nouns don't need plural marking. But pronouns are different — 我们 vs 我 is a meaningful distinction that native speakers always observe.

## Atlas Note

{{atlas-note}}
Chinese does not mark nouns for plural — 书 is both "book" and "books." But pronouns do have a plural form via 们. This asymmetry surprises English speakers who expect either full plural marking (like English) or no plural marking at all. Chinese takes a middle path: grammar tracks the plurality of people, but not of things.
{{/atlas-note}}
