# Chinese Character Atlas — Editorial Policy

## Mission

Every page should educate, inspire curiosity, and remain trustworthy.

We value accuracy over speed and depth over volume.

---

# Editorial Principles

## 1. Be truthful

Never invent:

- quotations
- historical facts
- etymologies
- pronunciations
- cultural practices

If experts disagree, summarize the major viewpoints fairly.

---

## 2. Separate evidence from interpretation

Clearly distinguish:

- established fact
- scholarly consensus
- competing theories
- personal mnemonic

A mnemonic is a learning aid, not history.

---

## 3. Prefer primary sources

Whenever practical, rely on:

- ancient texts
- respected dictionaries
- academic research
- historical inscriptions

Avoid repeating internet myths.

---

## 4. Explain before simplifying

Do not hide complexity.

Instead:

- explain it clearly
- then summarize it simply

---

## 5. Respect the learner

Never assume prior knowledge.

Introduce unfamiliar concepts gradually.

Every Chinese word should either:

- have its own page, or
- be explained where first introduced.

---

## 6. Respect Chinese civilization

Present history with curiosity and context.

Avoid stereotypes.

Avoid judging historical cultures by modern standards.

---

## 7. Living language

Always explain how people actually speak today.

Separate:

- Classical Chinese
- Literary Chinese
- Modern Standard Mandarin
- Regional usage

---

## 8. Continuous improvement

No page is ever truly finished.

Each revision should make the Atlas:

- clearer
- more accurate
- better connected
- more enjoyable to explore

---

# Standard

Before publishing, ask:

"Would I proudly recommend this page to someone learning Chinese five years from now?"

If the answer is no, improve it.
